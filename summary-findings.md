
# What I Found in Somalia's WTO Trade Assessment

I went through the files. The short version: Somalia has a few 
things working, but most are half-done or missing. And nearly 
every future deadline ties back to joining the East African 
Community in 2027.

---

## What Actually Works

A few measures are fully in place:

- The NTFC committee exists and meets with traders
- Customs rules for detention and notification are written
- You can submit cargo manifests before the ship arrives (SOMCAS system)
- Risk management works — green, yellow, red lanes
- Customs brokers are mandatory and licensed

That is about it. Everything else is either "partially" or "no."

---

## What "Partially" Means in Real Life

"Partially" usually means the law is on paper, or a website 
exists, but the information is old, no one updates it, or 
traders do not know about it. The trade portal is a shell — 
content just sits there.

---

## Three Biggest Gaps That Hurt Traders

**No advance rulings.**
You cannot ask customs ahead of time how they will classify 
your product. Every shipment is a gamble. The law has nothing 
on this.

**No testing labs.**
Somalia has no government lab to check if goods meet standards. 
Bad imports get in, and Somali exports cannot prove quality. 
For a livestock-exporting country, that is serious.

**No single window, no re-export.**
Traders deal with customs, health, and standards separately — 
multiple visits, multiple delays. If your goods are rejected, 
you cannot re-export them. You lose everything. Auction or 
destruction only.

---

## The Hidden Backbone — EAC Integration

What struck me most: Somalia is not building most of this on 
its own. Almost every future plan points to 2027, when it will 
fully join the EAC Single Customs Territory.

Advance rulings? Coming from EAC law.
Appeals procedure? From EAC law.
Authorized operators and transit rules? From EAC.

Without EAC, Somalia has no legal framework for these measures. 
So the real compliance timeline is EAC's — not Somalia's.
